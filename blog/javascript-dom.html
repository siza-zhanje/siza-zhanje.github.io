JavaScript relationship to HTML and CSS

Any car will take you from point A to point B. It is a question of how you get there. A car with a small engine (less than 2 litre petrol tank), going up hilly parts of the road may be slow and overtaking at the same time, a challenge.

This would be HTML, Hyper Text Markup Language. When a webpage is clicked, content is opened up and viewed with minimal formatting and layout, enabled by Markup Language - just enough to display the content and layout. Nothing more.

A bonus from work allows you to change the body paint to any color of your choice, add mag wheels, lower the suspension, tint the windows so that you drive incognito if you wish and maybe that exhaust for extra sound! Now you car is attention grabbing. This is CSS, Cascading Style Sheets.

Unfortunately you still have to crane your neck to reverse or parallel park in your tinted windows. Inside your car is a space next to the console where a display screen could be installed. Further, the boot is such that a small camera could be added and linked to the display screen at the console. This now means that when you engage the reverse gear, the camera switches on and the display screen shows you the boundaries for safely driving backwards. This ability to add elements (camera and display) to existing HTML & CSS plus the appropriate functionality is JavaScript. It enhances the user experience.

Control flow and loops, arrays, and objects

When writing lines of code in a program, the order in which these are read by the computer is described as Control Flow. Normally, the computer reads the lines, also called statements, from left-to-right; top-to-bottom.

Loops represent decisions within control flow. A loop is just that: it has a starting point and and ending point. In between the start and end points, the outcome of decisions made, determines the path taken to reach the decision.
